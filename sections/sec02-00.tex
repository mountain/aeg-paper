This section fixes the elementary language used throughout the paper. We begin with ordinary arithmetic expressions over $\mathbb{Q}$, represent them as trees, and isolate the special class of threadlike expressions. We then introduce the addition--multiplication grid as the first geometric model on which these expressions can be seen as paths. The emphasis here is deliberately pedagogical: before discussing flow, torsion, or contact geometry, we want the reader to have a small stock of explicit examples that can be carried mentally through the rest of the paper.

\subsection{Arithmetic expression}\label{subsec:arithmetic-expression}

To keep the first layer of the theory elementary, we start with arithmetic expressions over the rational numbers $\mathbb{Q}$. This avoids the analytic subtleties of real numbers while preserving the essential combinatorial features of expression building and evaluation.

\begin{definition}\label{def:arithmetic-expression}
An arithmetic expression $a$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ is generated by the production rules
\begin{equation}\label{eq:productionrule}
\begin{aligned}
a &\longleftarrow x, \\
a &\longleftarrow (a+a), \\
a &\longleftarrow (a-a), \\
a &\longleftarrow (a\times a), \\
a &\longleftarrow (a\div a),
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $x\in\mathbb{Q}$. We write $a\in \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{Q}]$.
\end{definition}

The same expression can be viewed in two equivalent ways:
\begin{itemize}
    \item as a \emph{string}, obtained by applying the production rules formally;
    \item as a \emph{syntax tree}, whose leaves are numbers and whose branch nodes are labeled by the operators $+, -, \times, \div$.
\end{itemize}

For example, consider the expression
\begin{equation}
(((((1 \times 2) \times 2) - 1) \times (2 + 1)) - 6).
\label{eq:equation}
\end{equation}
Its tree representation is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:syntaxtree}.

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\resizebox{0.2\textheight}{!}{\includegraphics{images/02-example-expression-syntax-tree.pdf}}
\caption{A tree representation of an arithmetic expression.}
\label{fig:syntaxtree}
\end{figure}

A \emph{sub-expression} is simply the expression determined by a subtree. Passing from the tree to the string is routine: one may perform a pre-order traversal and insert parentheses in the usual way.

Evaluation is a partial function
\[
\nu: \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{Q}] \rightarrow \mathbb{Q},
\]
undefined exactly when a division by zero occurs during the recursive evaluation process. It is given inductively by
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\nu(x)=x$ for any constant leaf $x\in\mathbb{Q}$;
    \item $\nu((a+b))=\nu(a)+\nu(b)$;
    \item $\nu((a-b))=\nu(a)-\nu(b)$;
    \item $\nu((a\times b))=\nu(a)\nu(b)$;
    \item $\nu((a\div b))=\nu(a)/\nu(b)$ whenever $\nu(b)\neq 0$.
\end{itemize}

We call $a$ \emph{evaluable} if $\nu(a)$ is defined. Unless explicitly stated otherwise, expressions in this paper are assumed to be evaluable.

Even when the final value is fixed, the intermediate order of evaluation need not be unique.

\begin{definition}
The \emph{evaluation order} of an arithmetic expression $a$ is an ordering of the branch nodes in its tree representation such that each node is evaluated only after all of its children have been evaluated.
\end{definition}

For the expression in \eqref{eq:equation}, the following are all legal evaluation orders:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $1 \times 2 \rightarrow \underline{2};\ \underline{2} \times 2 \rightarrow \underline{4};\ \underline{4} - 1 \rightarrow \underline{3};\ 2 + 1 \rightarrow \underline{3};\ \underline{3} \times \underline{3} \rightarrow \underline{9};\ \underline{9} - 6 \rightarrow 3$;
    \item $1 \times 2 \rightarrow \underline{2};\ \underline{2} \times 2 \rightarrow \underline{4};\ 2 + 1 \rightarrow \underline{3};\ \underline{4} - 1 \rightarrow \underline{3};\ \underline{3} \times \underline{3} \rightarrow \underline{9};\ \underline{9} - 6 \rightarrow 3$;
    \item $1 \times 2 \rightarrow \underline{2};\ 2 + 1 \rightarrow \underline{3};\ \underline{2} \times 2 \rightarrow \underline{4};\ \underline{4} - 1 \rightarrow \underline{3};\ \underline{3} \times \underline{3} \rightarrow \underline{9};\ \underline{9} - 6 \rightarrow 3$;
    \item $2 + 1 \rightarrow \underline{3};\ 1 \times 2 \rightarrow \underline{2};\ \underline{2} \times 2 \rightarrow \underline{4};\ \underline{4} - 1 \rightarrow \underline{3};\ \underline{3} \times \underline{3} \rightarrow \underline{9};\ \underline{9} - 6 \rightarrow 3$.
\end{itemize}
The underlined numbers are the intermediate values created during evaluation. This elementary example already shows one of the recurring themes of AEG: a single final value may conceal several distinct evaluation histories.

There are also important classes of expressions whose evaluation order is unique. Examples include right-expanded expressions, left-expanded expressions, and mixtures of the two, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:leftright} and Figure~\ref{fig:combination}.

\begin{definition}
A \emph{threadlike expression} is an arithmetic expression whose tree representation has the property that every left child is a leaf.
\end{definition}

Intuitively, a threadlike expression is built by starting from one initial number and then repeatedly attaching a new operation and a new number on the right. Such expressions are especially important because they admit a unique evaluation order and therefore behave like directed histories. Later they will become the prototype of geometric paths.

\subsection{Currying and path notation}\label{subsec:currying}

A threadlike expression can be read from left to right as a succession of unary operations acting on the current value. This viewpoint is the most convenient one for geometry.

We therefore introduce the operators
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\oplus_y : x \mapsto x+y$,
    \item $\ominus_y : x \mapsto x-y$,
    \item $\otimes_y : x \mapsto x\cdot e^y$,
    \item $\oslash_y : x \mapsto x\cdot e^{-y}$.
\end{itemize}

For example, the threadlike expression
\[
(((((1 \times 2) \times 2) + 1) \times 3) + 6)
\]
may be rewritten as
\[
\oplus_6\bigl(\otimes_{\ln 3}(\oplus_1(\otimes_{\ln 2}(\otimes_{\ln 2}(1))))\bigr).
\]

This motivates the following path notation. For operators $a_1, a_2, \dots, a_n$, define
\[
x a_1 a_2 \cdots a_n \coloneqq a_n\bigl(a_{n-1}(\cdots a_2(a_1(x))\cdots)\bigr).
\]
The same example then becomes simply
\[
1\,\otimes_{\ln 2}\,\otimes_{\ln 2}\,\oplus_1\,\otimes_{\ln 3}\,\oplus_6.
\]

If a path begins with a number, we call it a \emph{bounded path}. If it consists only of operators, we call it a \emph{free path}. A bounded path evaluates to a number; a free path evaluates to a function.

\begin{lemma}\label{lemma:associative}
The path notation is associative. In other words,
\[
a[bc]=[ab]c.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For a free path,
\[
a[bc]=[bc](\mathbf a)=\mathbf c(\mathbf b(\mathbf a)),
\qquad
[ab]c=\mathbf c([ab])=\mathbf c(\mathbf b(\mathbf a)).
\]
Hence $a[bc]=[ab]c$.

For a bounded path,
\[
x a[bc]=[bc](\mathbf a(x))=\mathbf c(\mathbf b(\mathbf a(x))),
\qquad
x[ab]c=\mathbf c([ab](x))=\mathbf c(\mathbf b(\mathbf a(x))).
\]
Again $a[bc]=[ab]c$ follows.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}\label{definition:concatenate}
The concatenation of two paths $p_1$ and $p_2$ is their composite as functions:
\[
p_1\cdot p_2 \coloneqq p_2\circ p_1.
\]
\end{definition}

Only the first factor in a concatenation may be bounded. If the first factor is bounded, the result is bounded; otherwise the result remains a free path.

\subsection{A scalar field and a mesh grid}\label{subsec:meshgrid}

Having described threadlike expressions syntactically, we now introduce the simplest geometric picture in which they can be drawn. Consider the upper half-plane
\[
\mathcal H=\{(x,y)\mid y>0\}
\]
equipped with the metric
\[
ds^2=\frac{1}{y^2}\left(dx^2+\frac{dy^2}{\ln^2 2}\right)
\]
and the assignment function
\begin{equation}
a=-\frac{x}{y}.
\label{eq:assignment}
\end{equation}
We call $a$ the \emph{assignment}.

At this stage the reader may treat $(\mathcal H,a)$ simply as a prototype. Later sections justify why this choice is natural, but for now the main point is concrete: it supplies a grid on which ``add $1$'' and ``multiply by $2$'' become visible motions.

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\resizebox{0.9\textwidth}{!}{\includegraphics{images/01-grid-example-1.pdf}}
\caption{An addition--multiplication grid with generators $\mu=1$ and $\lambda=\ln 2$.}
\label{fig:gridex0}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:gridex0} shows the resulting addition--multiplication grid. The blue family represents the relation ``$+1$,'' the green family represents ``$\times 2$,'' and the red labels record the assignment values at the crossing points. The two curve families are perpendicular, and the neighboring grid steps have unit length with respect to the above metric.

For the purposes of this section, the picture may be read informally as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item moving horizontally changes the current value by addition or subtraction;
    \item moving vertically changes the current value by multiplication or division;
    \item a threadlike expression becomes a zigzag path on the grid.
\end{itemize}

This is the first point where the paper begins to look geometric rather than merely symbolic.

\subsection{Encoding threadlike expressions on the addition--multiplication grid}\label{subsec:encoding}

Once the grid is available, threadlike expressions can be drawn directly on it. In Figure~\ref{fig:encoding}, the bold black path encodes the expression
\[
((((1\times 4)-1)\times 2)-3).
\]

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\resizebox{0.9\textwidth}{!}{\includegraphics{images/05-example-expression-embedding}}
\caption{Encoding a threadlike expression as a zigzag path.}
\label{fig:encoding}
\end{figure}

Reading the path segment by segment, we obtain:
\begin{itemize}
    \item the vertical segment from $1$ to $4$: multiplication by $4$;
    \item the horizontal segment from $4$ to $3$: subtraction by $1$;
    \item the vertical segment from $3$ to $6$: multiplication by $2$;
    \item the horizontal segment from $6$ to $3$: subtraction by $3$.
\end{itemize}

This example is elementary, but it is worth lingering over. The path is not merely a picture of the final value $3$; it is a record of how the value $3$ was obtained.

\subsection{From a scalar field to a space of threadlike expressions}\label{subsec:from-field-to-space}

The next example is more revealing. In Figure~\ref{fig:canonicalform}, several different paths start at the same source value $1$ and end at the same target value $3$.

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\resizebox{0.9\textwidth}{!}{\includegraphics{images/06-example-canonical-form}}
\caption{Different encodings of the same endpoint relation.}
\label{fig:canonicalform}
\end{figure}

The displayed paths correspond to:
\begin{itemize}
    \item the black path: $((1\times 8)-5)=3$;
    \item the purple path: $((1-\frac{5}{8})\times 8)=3$;
    \item the brown path: $((((((1-\frac18)\times 2)-\frac12)\times 2)-1)\times 2)=3$;
    \item the orange path: an infinite accumulation of addition and multiplication steps.
\end{itemize}

The black, purple, and brown paths all have the same source and the same target, yet they record different histories. Algebraically, they are related by distributivity and by regrouping of terms.

For example, the brown path can be compressed to the black path:
\begin{align}
3 &= ((((((1-\tfrac18)\times 2)-\tfrac12)\times 2)-1)\times 2) \\
  &= 1\times 8 - \tfrac18\times 8 - \tfrac12\times 4 - 1\times 2 \\
  &= (1\times 8)-5.
\end{align}

The same brown path can also be reorganized into the purple path:
\begin{align}
3 &= ((((((1-\tfrac18)\times 2)-\tfrac12)\times 2)-1)\times 2) \\
  &= (1-\tfrac18)\times 8 - \tfrac12\times 4 - 1\times 2 \\
  &= (1-\tfrac18)\times 8 - \tfrac14\times 8 - \tfrac14\times 8 \\
  &= \bigl(1-\tfrac18-\tfrac14-\tfrac14\bigr)\times 8 \\
  &= \bigl(1-\tfrac58\bigr)\times 8.
\end{align}

We may therefore regard the black and purple paths as a first \emph{canonical pair}: one emphasizes the final multiplication, the other emphasizes the total additive correction before multiplication.

Pedagogically, this is one of the most important examples in the section. It shows that a space of expressions should not be thought of as a mere list of values. What matters is the coexistence of multiple histories between the same endpoints. Later, torsion will measure the failure of such histories to collapse completely.

If one prefers categorical language, the same picture may be packaged by taking grid points as objects and encoded paths as morphisms, with composition given by concatenation. We do not need the full groupoid formalism in the present paper, but the path viewpoint will remain essential.
